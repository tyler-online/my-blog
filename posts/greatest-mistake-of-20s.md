---
title: "The greatest mistake (of my 20s)"
date: "May 13, 2026"
---



The greatest mistake of my 20s was that I viewed time as money, and money as time.

In the traditional view (that I too often fell susceptible to):

* Money is finite, and I should expend significant effort figuring out how to acquire it.
* Time is infinite, and I should only expend a little effort to ensure it’s not being wasted.

In reality:

* There is an infinite amount of money out there (more than I could ever spend, or even want), and I should only use a bit of mental bandwidth on financial hygiene.
* Time is limited \& hugely constrained, both on a daily basis and in the totality of life. I should be very judicious with my time. I should wake up every day and view each hour as a “token” I can spend. After a healthy 8 hours of sleep, I have 16 remaining tokens to spend. It’s worth thinking hard about how to spend those tokens.

I now think the right approach is to try to understand what is important to you, frequently audit \& rebalance how you allocate your 16 daily “time tokens” to be in alignment with what is important, and forget the rest. If your life’s mission isn’t clear to you (it certainly wasn’t for me in my early 20s), then a great use of these tokens is to experiment to figure out what you are interested in. From interest develops passion, and from passion develops purpose.

The consequences of not doing so are dire. The (embarrassing) truth is, I’m still in the “figuring out my life’s core mission” phase – and that’s a direct consequence of the backwards view of time and money I previously had. I’ve had some success, but I haven’t focused enough on impact. I haven’t found out how big a fish I am. I haven’t chased a category-defining problem or era-defining innovation.

But the beauty of these tokens is that they are immensely valuable and fungible, so they can be spent on anything! Their value is entirely relative to whether or not they bring you closer to the life you want.

It sounds simple, but I don’t think it is. And I don’t think it’s meant to be. Most people create a coherent narrative for their life only in retrospect, when it’s easy to connect the dots – but the reality is, they were often uncertain as well.

Also, some things are worth doing because they feel a part of you, even if they may not lead to some grand vision of the highest version of who you are. For example, I feel well-suited for and interested in commerce. It was always a bucket list item for me to start a business. Participating in markets is fun, rewarding, and prosocial. You get to implement new ideas, see if people like \& benefit from your product, and have the freedom to determine how to spend your time.

Despite not feeling like it brought me much closer to my “ideal self” or career goals, I don’t regret starting a skincare company because it ticked off 2 bucket list items for me, and I’ve learned a lot. But I probably spent too long at my last job before the skincare company “learning” how to run a consumer brand (I spent too many of my life’s “time tokens” there from a macro perspective). And I probably made some changes too slowly at my skincare brand (I spent too many of my daily “time tokens” on deliberation, from a micro perspective).

I think a good heuristic of whether you are implementing the “time token” analogy correctly is whether it increases the rate at which you take action, or decreases it. If you are still figuring out your purpose, it should increase your rate of action. If you already have, then it will likely decrease your rate of action (at least on extraneous things).

Common failure modes:

* Thinking so much you never do anything
* Working so hard for money you miss the point of life, or your purpose
* Not undertaking a venture because “it will take too long,” and time is limited. Time will pass anyways, so just do what you care about. Anything less is time wasted.
* Caring so little about money that you abandon strategy completely (leverage still matters; h/t Naval)
* Spending time tokens on some grand vision that sounds good, but has not been pressure tested
