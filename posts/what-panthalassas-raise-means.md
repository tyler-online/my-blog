---
title: "What Panthalassa's raise means (the NIMBYs won)"
date: "May 14, 2026"
---

Panthalassa is an incredibly cool company, and its recent $140M raise led by Peter Thiel is perhaps even more interesting because of what it means.

A quick primer:
Panthalassa builds massive, floating, autonomous steel structures that they call “nodes.” These nodes get placed in the ocean, autonomously navigate to designated locations, and start generating energy from waves. This energy is used to power onboard chips that complete AI inference tasks, then transmit the outputs to satellites, which in turn transmit the data wherever it needs to go. From waves to insights, and from sea to space to land! It’s super cool.

It also sounds crazy. And it kind of is.

Wave energy is not very efficient. Gas, solar, and nuclear are all more cost effective. But that’s the thing: AI datacenters are no longer just about cost per electron. That view ignores time-to-power and permission-to-build.

In the US:
- Grid interconnection queues are lengthening
- Local politics increasingly opposes datacenter construction
- People wrongly believe that datacenters consume environmentally-harmful amounts of water for cooling purposes
- Solar can be difficult to colocate with datacenters
- Nuclear takes a long time to build

In contrast, Panthalassa:
- Does not require grid connections
- Does not have to deal with local politics
- Has abundant & free water for cooling
- Integrates power generation & compute
- Is rate-limited only by how fast its factory can build nodes

Panthalassa “goes where the energy is” and, more importantly, goes where the NIMBYs are not. I think Thiel has basically looked at the risk-reward of terrestrial datacenters and sees increasing backlash resulting in higher costs. So he’s decided to bet on a relatively permissionless alternative: He’s betting that Panthalassa can get to economies of scale and bend the cost curve down to the point where marginal cost per electron generated is not the deciding factor in datacenter economics. And I think that’s smart: either we get factories that can churn out vertically-integrated datacenters at scale, or he gets a relatively inexpensive hedge that turned out to be wrong.

By the way, it’s definitely not a shoe-in. Marine O&M is notoriously difficult. Wave energy is still immature, intermittent, and seasonal. Compute maintenance isn’t exactly hands-off. Satellite bandwidth isn’t cheap. There are still a lot of hurdles for Panthalassa to overcome, but I can’t help but picture “fields” of their nodes floating in the middle of the ocean under starlight, helping solve important tasks, and hope that they succeed – despite what that would imply for terrestrial datacenters.
