---
title: "A noble desire that fails in practice"
date: "May 30, 2026"
---

Life coaches are either subtly maligned or exalted, depending on which social groups you’re in. I see it a little differently.

I think the core drive of most life coaches is noble: they want to help people, they want others’ potential to be realized, and they want to live a fulfilling life.

The problem is in the execution of those desires, which – in the case of life coaches – tends to be a sort of “pyramid scheme of meaning.” Here’s what I mean: It’s very difficult to figure out your life’s purpose*. It’s a lot easier to make your purpose helping someone else bring their purpose to fruition. This is because you don’t have to wrestle with the weight of self-doubt, failure, or confusion** – all you have to do is provide encouragement.

Of course, life coaches do more than just say “you can do it,” over and over again – but the point stands. They generally fall into one of several buckets:

1. The young person who is struggling to find their place in the world, and wants to vaguely “help others.” These types gravitate towards surf towns, remote work, and indiscriminate good vibes. Their primary function is to help you quiet your fears and manufacture self-belief. The “treatment” probably wears off quickly.  
2. The therapist-coach. These are probably quite useful to the type of person who doesn’t want to go to therapy but needs a second opinion & an ear to listen. The coach probably provides therapy disguised as “coaching,” and it may even be useful.  
3. The executive-coach. These are the extreme example of the therapist-coach, catered to extremely high-drive, Type A executives. The executives basically don’t have time or a proper outlet for therapy/work-life optimization, and the executive-coach gives them the time & space to do so. These coaches are probably high IQ enough to not be immediately dismissed as charlatans.  
4. The outlier. Mark Twain once said, “An expert is an ordinary fellow from another town." It’s easier to believe someone is an expert or guru if they also look the part. These life coaches probably have an accent, or dress oddly, or are super tall or super short, or grew up in a unique way – something has to be visibility, undeniably different. Of course, this doesn’t actually make them a more qualified life coach; but it may increase their efficacy by virtue of giving people a stronger sense of belief in them that is self-reinforcing (a placebo effect).  
5. The businessman. These are the wealthy life coaches that have turned their coaching into an optimized funnel. They are probably digitally fluent. They are probably less concerned with driving outcomes for their customers (except as content for marketing). These aren’t real life coaches; they’re more akin to SMB entrepreneurs.

I actually don’t have anything against life coaches, and this blog isn’t meant to unfairly malign them. I just think one should know what they’re getting into when it comes to life coaches. They may have utility, they may not.

My recommendation for life coaches, or anyone else with the noble desire of wanting to help others, is this: be specific. Resist the urge to be an indefinite optimist. Hold yourself accountable to really fight through several experiments & failures on the path to finding your purpose. Channel your good will into specific causes that resonate – like mentoring youth, fostering animals, investing in someone early in their career, starting an organization with a specific goal, or building something new. I think those will be far more rewarding in the long-run.

Capitalism is great. Charity is great. Capitalism masquerading as charity is not.  
Investing is great. Making an impact is great. Investing masquerading as impact is not.   
Coaching is great. Living a fulfilling life is great. Life design masquerading as coaching is not.















*It’s even harder to bring that purpose to fruition once you’ve discovered it. And I think those two things are more related than we generally acknowledge; that is, passion & purpose tend to develop when you are having success at something you’re interested in. So, you may initially think something is your life’s purpose, but whether you still feel that way down the line probably has more to do with whether you are succeeding or not. Those who succeed were “right,” and those who don’t move onto another “life’s purpose.” At some point, it’s tempting to stop jumping to the next attempt, and to instead become a life coach.

**Of course, life coaches probably struggle with these things too. But it’s a different flavor of struggle. If you are in pursuit of a specific dream, those struggles come from trying to bring something new into the world; from coming into contact with the reality of market feedback (“Does my product work?”; “Am I a good enough singer to make a living from this?”; “Am I really willing to risk it all for this?”). If you are a life coach struggling with these things, it may feel the same and even some of the questions may be the same – but consider if everyone had the same approach. We’d all just be coaching coaches who were trying to “live their dream” and “see if they were good enough” to build their “dream life” by coaching other coaches doing the same thing. I’d posit that a lot of the self-doubt and confusion life coaches may experience comes from not actually having a specific & defined purpose; just a generalized good will.
