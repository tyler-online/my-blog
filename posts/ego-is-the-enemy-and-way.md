---
title: "Ego is the enemy (and the way)"
date: "May 30, 2026"
---

In the post-Ryan Holiday era, discourse on ego seems to center on it being either a spiritual delusion (Buddhism) or a pragmatic hindrance (Stoicism).

I think it just depends on what “mode” you’re in.

On the pragmatic side:
Shrinking the ego can be good for learning. This prevents embarrassment from stopping you asking “stupid” questions or trying to build things that might fail, thereby accelerating your learning.

However, expanding the ego could be good for execution. If you’re fearful of looking incompetent, you might go the extra mile on a project.

On the spiritual side:
I tend to think shrinking the ego is good insofar as it dulls one’s sensitivity to the societal pressure of what they “should” be doing. If you’re not worried about looking dumb or different, then you’re probably more likely to follow your genuine enthusiasms. If you’re interested in something, you’re probably good at it; and if you’re good at something and interested in it, it’s probably a spiritually worthy use of your time.

However, many of the greats have strived to be the best. That comes from a place of ego, but has produced beautiful things. And if it’s something that was spiritually aligned for them, I don’t think that’s a bad thing. It’s clear Beethoven was meant to compose, Michelangelo was meant to create art, and Newton was meant to study science – but all three of them (and many other greats) cared a great deal about being the best.
