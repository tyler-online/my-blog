---
title: "Learning vs. knowing vs. feeling"
date: "May 13, 2026"
---

I previously wrote about the biggest mistake of my 20s. The lessons I learned weren’t particularly novel, so why did I make that mistake?

By the time I made my mistake, billionaire Bill Perkins had already said “If I could trade all of my wealth right now to be 30 years old again, I would do it in a heartbeat.” Warren Buffett had also expressed similar sentiments. I was aware of their quotes, so (again) why did I make the mistake?

I just think cliches don’t get through until you learn the underlying lesson for yourself through experience. Then you feel it, not just hear it.

When we say we “know” something, what we usually mean is that we read it or heard it somewhere. But “knowing” or “learning” something implies that you’ve integrated the idea/lesson, and oftentimes we haven’t.

Perhaps I’m just a particularly physically embodied person, but I suspect our biology is better at understanding sense-based communication. I’m going to experiment with changing my vocabulary from saying I “know” something to saying I “heard” (if someone told me) or “saw” (if I read it). Only earned insights from direct experience will get labeled as “knowing” or “feeling.”

This seems like it would absolve one of the false impression they've learned something when they really haven't.
