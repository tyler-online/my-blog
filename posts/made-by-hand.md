---
title: "We still know how to do this for now..."
date: "May 14, 2026"
---

The SR-71 Blackbird is unfathomably cool. It looks phenomenal. It’s the fastest air-breathing manned craft ever (still, despite being designed in the late 1950s / early 1960s). And it was designed by hand with a slide rule, before we had computers.

I think that’s hard for today’s engineers to imagine doing, and I wonder what else will be like that in the future. Which fields will be so transformed by AI, so fast, that 10-20 years from now the foremost experts in the fields will be unable to replicate what their predecessors created by hand decades ago?

Programming is the obvious answer, but I think the film industry is a bigger one. There’s just such a huge price premium on creating things in the physical world, and movies have historically required an insane amount of it. Set designers, costume designers, composers, soundscapes, voice actors, etc. Entire networks of people experimenting and exchanging knowledge that will one day be lost. Digital processes have already changed the field a lot, but AI finally tips the scale: The token cost curve has just been driven down too far for the industry to support the wages of multiple people whose expertise is creating miniature photorealistic fantasy orc cities (and other esoteric knowledge).

PS – Honorable mention to the video game industry. The TL;DR is that there’s an argument to be made that graphics haven’t improved much in the last 10 years despite massive model & chip improvements; primitive tech forced the best designers from 10 years ago to get creative in ways we’ve already lost (or soon will).

PPS – By the way, this isn’t a commentary on what I think is “best for society.” It’s me calling balls and strikes as I see them.
